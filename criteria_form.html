About the Delphi method

The Delphi method is a structured, iterative research technique designed to reach expert consensus on complex topics where direct empirical evidence is limited or where professional judgment plays a central role. Originally developed at the RAND Corporation in the 1950s, it has since become a widely accepted approach in healthcare, policy development, and clinical guideline formation.

The process works through a series of questionnaire rounds. Experts respond independently, which removes the influence of dominant voices and encourages honest, unbiased input. After each round, the facilitator compiles and shares a summary of the group's responses. Participants then review this collective feedback and have the opportunity to revise their own positions in the following round. This cycle of response, reflection, and revision continues until the group reaches a stable level of agreement — known as consensus.

In this study, three rounds will be conducted. Each round builds on the previous one, progressively narrowing the range of expert opinion and identifying both areas of strong agreement and areas requiring further discussion.

Your responses in each round are compiled and reviewed only by the research facilitator. While your name and professional details are collected to allow the facilitator to follow up with you directly for clarification between rounds, your individual responses will not be attributed to you or shared with other panel members. Only anonymised, aggregated group summaries will be circulated as feedback between rounds.

MOHE facilities. Decision makers include procurement personnel and tender committee members.

Users
Decision makers will use this to evaluate bids to identify the OPP of highest value.
Scope
Multi-stakeholder (physician, pharmacist, procurement personnel), for the annual tender process (time horizon), considering both technical and financial performances.
Evidence
ISPOR good practice guidelines, MCDA guidances, and current scoring scheme.
